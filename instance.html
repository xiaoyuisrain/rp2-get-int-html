Replacing the target word

You could use words or phrases in place of the target word, and/or insert words between any of the non-target words in the text, as long as the non-target words retain their original word form and word order, and the resulting paraphrase sounds natural and is grammatically acceptable.

For example, to paraphrase the sentence below:

  • The artist has captured her perfectly.

You could insert one or more words in one or all of the blanks:

  • the artist has her perfectly .

The following changes will not be accepted:

  • The artist has her precisely.
  • She has been by the artist.

If our algorithm thinks your paraphrase modifies any non-target words, it will display a warning message when you click the Proceed button. Please click the button again everytime you change your paraphrase.

Please also avoid using words in which the target word is somewhat present. For example, please do not substitute the verb connect with phrases containing connection or disconnect; inflected forms (connects, connected, and connecting) will not be accepted either.

Literal language

A sentence uses literal language if it expresses meaning directly and only contains concepts belonging to the topic that the sentence is about. In particular, it does not use concrete words if the context is abstract or the other way around.

Examples of literal sentences:

  • I have to go to my country to see my father.
  • Turnbull busies himself with his clipboard as his reply is translated.
  • The diagrams are updated periodically during the course of the simulation.

Examples of non-literal sentences:

  • And tell me, sweet creature, do you count as a toy?
  • No longer can the Government disguise the crisis surrounding electricity privatisation.
  • I wouldn't want her to have a rough time.
