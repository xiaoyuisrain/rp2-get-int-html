Full instructions for the paraphrasing task

Your task is to paraphrase the given text by replacing the emphasised word (the target word) in the text. Your paraphrase should meet all the requirements specified below.

Replacing the target word

You could substitute the target word with one or more words and/or insert words anywhere in the text, as long as:

  1. the target word is not present in your paraphrase,
  2. the non-target words retain their original word form and word order, and
  3. the resulting paraphrase sounds natural and is grammatically acceptable.

For example, given the following sentence, in which captured is the target word:

  • The artist has captured her perfectly.

You could consider the following modifications:*

  • The artist has described her perfectly in the picture.
  • The artist has completed a painting that portrays her perfectly.
  • In this picture, the artist has shown the characteristics of her perfectly.

The following paraphrases will be rejected directly:

  • The artist has captured her precisely.
  • The artist has provided a perfect portrait of her perfectly.
  • She has been pictured perfectly by the artist.

*: These examples are meant to give you an idea of what types of modifications are allowed. They by no means imply what we regard as apt and literal paraphrases (see the section on literalness vs aptness below).

If our algorithm thinks your paraphrase modifies any non-target words, it will display a warning message when you click the Proceed button. Please click the button again everytime you change your paraphrase.

Please also avoid using words in which the target word is somewhat present. For example, please do not substitute the verb connect with phrases containing connection or disconnect; inflected forms (connects, connected, and connecting) will not be accepted either.

Literalness vs aptness

The paraphrase you submit should be an apt paraphrase, i.e. the paraphrase should convey the meaning of the original text. The paraphrase should also use literal language. A sentence uses literal language if it conveys meaning directly, describing an object or an event as it is, and using words that are typically associated with the object or event. Literal language constrasts with figurative language, which can take the form of metaphor, personification, simile, idiom, pun, hyperbole, etc.

Examples of literal sentences:

  • I have to go to my country to see my father.
  • Turnbull busies himself with his clipboard as his reply is translated.
  • The diagrams are updated periodically during the course of the simulation.

Examples of non-literal sentences:

  • And tell me, sweet creature, do you count as a toy?
    • Sweet refers to a sugary taste. As it is unlikely that the person being referred to as sweet creature has a sugary taste, the usage is non-literal.
    • People are not toys, so the comparison between the person and toys is also non-literal.
    • Count refers to an abstract thinking process in this sentence, of considering whether a person belongs to a particular category. As the word has a more concrete meaning of calculating the number of objects, its usage here is non-literal.
  • No longer can the Government disguise the crisis surrounding electricity privatisation.
    Both disguise and surround have a more concrete meaning than their meaning in this sentence.
  • I wouldn't want her to have a rough time.
    Rough refers to the texture of a surface, how the surface of something feels against your skin. Time apparently does not have a surface for us to touch; the usage is therefore non-literal.

Please note that the literalness of your paraphrase should be considered independently of the original text, and is therefore different from the aptness of the paraphrase. A paraphrase could use only literal language, but fail to convey the meaning of the original text. This paraphrase would be literal but inapt. On the other hand, a paraphrase could convey the meaning of the original text, but use non-literal language. This paraphrase would be apt but non-literal. Your aim is to provide paraphrases that do convey the meaning of the original text, and use only literal language; these paraphrases would be apt and literal. Of course, you may not be able to find the perfect literal and apt paraphrase. Just do the best you can, and keep the difference between literalness and aptness in mind when answering the rating questions.

Entering NA in the paraphrase field

If you think it is impossible for anyone to paraphrase the given text as instructed, please enter NA (without the quotation marks) and Proceed; you will be directed to provide a reason why you think the task is impossible. We value your opinion on impossible paraphrasing tasks as much as valid paraphrases of texts that can be paraphrased.

On the other hand, if you are worried about rejections, please do not accept the HIT or return it if you have already accepted it.

Please do not use NA, None, null, or other similar values in any other text areas.

Encountering the same target word

Please be cautious when accepting multiple HITs for the same target word (captured, captures and capture are considered the same word). We might have to reject your assignments if the same word is always paraphrased in the same way.

We recommend you to skip or return the HIT if you are unsure whether you have paraphrased the target word before.
